## 1. PartialEdit Dataset

### Source Paper

- **Title:** "PartialEdit: Identifying Partial Deepfakes in the Era of Neural Speech Editing"
- **Authors:** You Zhang, Baotong Tian, Lin Zhang, Zhiyao Duan
- **Year:** 2025 (camera-ready)
- **Venue:** Interspeech 2025
- **arXiv:** 2506.02958 (submitted June 3, 2025)
- **Dataset repository:** https://zenodo.org/records/15519188
- **Project page:** https://yzyouzhang.com/PartialEdit/

### Dataset Description

PartialEdit addresses a critical gap identified by the authors: existing partial spoof datasets (primarily PartialSpoof) use concatenation-based methods to splice TTS/VC segments into genuine speech, but real-world attackers increasingly use **neural speech editing** tools that modify speech in-place without splicing boundaries. Models trained on PartialSpoof fail to detect these edits.

### Dataset Size

- **1,000 utterances** drawn from the LibriSpeech test-clean corpus
- Source speakers from the VCTK dataset are also used for demonstration (speakers p232, p303, p247, p311, p256 shown on the project page)

### Types of Partial Spoofs

PartialEdit uses **neural speech editing methods** rather than concatenation-based splicing:

1. **E1 and E2** -- two distinct neural speech editing systems (the paper references VoiceCraft, A3T/Autoregressive Audio-to-Audio Transformer, and FluentSpeech as candidate systems)
2. **Codec variants** (E1-Codec, E2-Codec) -- versions processed through neural audio codecs

Three categories of manipulation:
- **Word replacement** -- substituting original words with different words
- **Word insertion** -- adding new words into utterances
- **Word deletion** -- removing words from the original speech

### Labels and Annotations

- **Binary segment-level labels** distinguishing authentic vs. edited regions
- Annotations track which specific portions of each utterance underwent editing
- Supports both **utterance-level detection** (is any part edited?) and **segment-level localization** (which frames are edited?)

### How It Differs from PartialSpoof

| Feature | PartialSpoof | PartialEdit |
|---------|-------------|-------------|
| **Manipulation method** | Concatenation-based (TTS/VC segments spliced in) | Neural speech editing (in-place modification) |
| **Boundary artifacts** | Present at splice points | Minimal or absent (seamless editing) |
| **Source** | ASVspoof 2019 LA TTS/VC systems | Modern neural editing (VoiceCraft, A3T, FluentSpeech) |
| **Edit granularity** | Segment replacement | Word-level replacement, insertion, deletion |
| **Detection challenge** | Boundary/transition artifacts detectable | No clear boundaries; content-level detection required |
| **Cross-dataset transfer** | Models trained on PartialSpoof fail on PartialEdit | Motivates need for editing-aware training |

### Key Finding

> "Models trained on the existing PartialSpoof dataset fail to detect partially edited speech generated by neural speech editing models."

This is a critical finding for forensic applications: as neural speech editing tools (e.g., VoiceCraft) become mainstream, splice-boundary-dependent detectors will become obsolete.

### Evaluation Metrics

- Utterance-level EER
- Segment-level EER
- F1-score (precision/recall balance across editing scenarios)

*(Specific baseline numbers require full paper access; the abstract and project page confirm these metrics are reported.)*

## 2. HQ-MPSD Dataset

### Source Paper

- **Full name:** "HQ-MPSD: A Multilingual Artifact-Controlled Benchmark for Partial Deepfake Speech Detection"
- **Authors:** Menglu Li, Majd Alber, Ramtin Asgarianamiri, Lian Zhao, Xiao-Ping Zhang
- **Affiliation:** Toronto Metropolitan University
- **Year:** 2025
- **arXiv:** 2512.13012 (submitted December 15, 2025)
- **Dataset repository:** https://zenodo.org/records/17929533 (CC-BY license)
- **Total data volume:** ~41.9 GB compressed, ~1.7 TB uncompressed

### Dataset Size

- **350.8 hours** of speech
- **550 speakers**
- **8 languages**

### Languages

| Language | Compressed Size |
|----------|----------------|
| English | 3.2 GB |
| French | 7.2 GB |
| German | 6.9 GB |
| Italian | 3.4 GB |
| Dutch | 1.1 GB |
| Polish | 8.8 GB |
| Portuguese | 3.4 GB |
| Spanish | 7.9 GB |

### Categories of Audio

1. **Bonafide** -- authentic speech
2. **Fully Fake** -- completely synthesized utterances
3. **Partial Fake Clean** -- partially deepfaked without background noise
4. **Partial Fake Noisy** -- partially deepfaked with noise augmentation (noise sources from OpenSLR26 and MUSAN databases)

### Labels and Annotations

- **Frame-level labeling at 30 ms intervals** with three categories:
  - **Genuine frames**
  - **Deepfake frames**
  - **Transition frames** (boundary regions between genuine and fake)
- The three-class labeling (genuine/deepfake/transition) is a distinguishing feature not found in PartialSpoof's binary scheme

### Unique Characteristics

1. **Artifact-controlled construction:** Uses linguistically coherent splice points derived from **fine-grained forced alignment** to preserve prosodic and semantic continuity, minimizing audible and visible boundary artifacts
2. **Multilingual scope:** 8 languages vs. PartialSpoof's English-only
3. **MOS validation:** Mean Opinion Score evaluations and spectrogram analysis confirm high perceptual naturalness
4. **Noise augmentation:** Realistic acoustic conditions via MUSAN/OpenSLR26
5. **Three-class frame labels:** Genuine/deepfake/transition (vs. binary in PartialSpoof)

### How It Compares to PartialSpoof

| Feature | PartialSpoof | HQ-MPSD |
|---------|-------------|---------|
| **Size** | ~41 hours | **350.8 hours** |
| **Languages** | English only | **8 languages** |
| **Speakers** | ~100 | **550** |
| **Frame label granularity** | 20-640 ms (6 resolutions) | **30 ms** |
| **Label classes** | Binary (real/fake) | **Ternary (genuine/deepfake/transition)** |
| **Noise augmentation** | No | **Yes (OpenSLR26, MUSAN)** |
| **Splice naturalness** | Standard concatenation | **Forced-alignment-based coherent splicing** |
| **Quality validation** | Not explicitly | **MOS + spectrogram analysis** |

### Key Benchmark Finding

> "State-of-the-art detection models show performance drops exceeding 80% on HQ-MPSD during cross-language and cross-dataset evaluations."

This demonstrates that when low-level concatenation artifacts are removed (through careful forced-alignment-based splicing), current detection systems fail dramatically -- highlighting the dataset's value as a stress test.

## 3. LlamaPartialSpoof Dataset

### Source Paper

- **Full name:** "LlamaPartialSpoof: An LLM-Driven Fake Speech Dataset Simulating Disinformation Generation"
- **Authors:** Hieu-Thi Luong, Haoyang Li, Lin Zhang, Kong Aik Lee, Eng Siong Chng
- **Year:** 2024
- **Venue:** ICASSP 2025 (accepted)
- **arXiv:** 2409.14743 (submitted September 23, 2024; revised January 6, 2025)

### Dataset Size

- **130 hours** of audio
- Contains both **fully fake** and **partially fake** speech

### Dataset Construction

This dataset uniquely uses an **LLM-driven attack pipeline**:

1. **Large Language Model (LLM)** -- used to generate semantically meaningful text modifications (simulating how a real attacker would use an LLM to craft disinformation by altering specific words/phrases)
2. **Voice cloning technologies** -- used to synthesize the modified speech segments
3. The pipeline simulates realistic disinformation generation where an attacker uses AI tools to modify specific claims within authentic speech recordings

### Types of Partial Spoofs

- **Fully fake utterances** -- entirely synthesized
- **Partially fake utterances** -- genuine speech with LLM-selected segments replaced by cloned speech
- Multiple **TTS models** and **concatenation methods** are used to create diversity

### Labels and Annotations

- Segment-level labels indicating which portions are genuine vs. synthetic
- Supports both utterance-level detection and segment-level localization tasks

### How It Differs from PartialSpoof

| Feature | PartialSpoof | LlamaPartialSpoof |
|---------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Size** | ~41 hours | **130 hours** |
| **Attack motivation** | Defender's perspective (test CM systems) | **Attacker's perspective (simulate disinformation)** |
| **Content selection** | Random segment replacement | **LLM-driven semantically motivated replacement** |
| **Voice cloning** | ASVspoof 2019 TTS/VC systems | **Modern voice cloning technology** |
| **Scenario realism** | Laboratory | **Real-world disinformation simulation** |

### Key Vulnerabilities Identified

The paper identifies exploitable weaknesses in current countermeasure (CM) systems:
- **Biases toward certain TTS models** -- CMs perform unevenly across different synthesis systems
- **Biases toward certain concatenation methods** -- specific splicing approaches are easier/harder to detect
- Attackers can exploit these biases to increase attack success rates

### Evaluation Results

- **Best CM performance: 24.49% EER** on unseen scenarios
- This is dramatically worse than the <1% EER achieved on in-domain PartialSpoof, demonstrating severe generalization failure

### Cross-Dataset Evaluation (from Luong et al., 2025, arXiv 2507.03468)

When a model (CFPRF) trained on PartialSpoof is evaluated on LlamaPartialSpoof:
- **In-domain (PartialSpoof):** 7.61% EER (20 ms)
- **Out-of-domain (LlamaPartialSpoof):** 43.25% EER
- **Out-of-domain (Half-Truth/HAD):** 27.59% EER

This confirms severe cross-dataset generalization failure.

## 4. Reference: Original PartialSpoof Database

### Source Papers

**Initial paper:**
- **Title:** "An Initial Investigation for Detecting Partially Spoofed Audio"
- **Authors:** Lin Zhang, Xin Wang, Erica Cooper, Junichi Yamagishi, Jose Patino, Nicholas Evans
- **Venue:** Interspeech 2021
- **arXiv:** 2104.02518

**Extended paper:**
- **Title:** "The PartialSpoof Database and Countermeasures for the Detection of Short Fake Speech Segments Embedded in an Utterance"
- **Authors:** Lin Zhang, Xin Wang, Erica Cooper, Nicholas Evans, Junichi Yamagishi
- **Venue:** IEEE/ACM Transactions on Audio, Speech, and Language Processing, 2023
- **arXiv:** 2204.05177
- **DOI:** 10.1109/TASLP.2022.3233236

### Dataset Specifications

- Built on top of **ASVspoof 2019 LA** data
- Contains utterances with **mixed genuine and spoofed segments** (TTS/VC segments spliced into genuine speech)
- **Segment-level labels at 6 temporal resolutions:** 20 ms, 40 ms, 80 ms, 160 ms, 320 ms, 640 ms
- Binary labels: real/fake per segment
- Also provides utterance-level labels
- Approximately **41 hours** of audio
- English only

### Baseline Results (from original paper)

- **Utterance-level EER:** 0.77% on PartialSpoof
- **Utterance-level EER:** 0.90% on ASVspoof 2019 LA
- Using self-supervised pre-trained feature extractors

## 5. Reference: HAD (Half-truth Audio Deepfake) Dataset

### Source Paper

- **Title:** "Half-Truth: A Partially Fake Audio Detection Dataset"
- **Authors:** Jiangyan Yi, Ye Bai, Jianhua Tao, Haoxin Ma, Zhengkun Tian, Chenglong Wang, Tao Wang, Ruibo Fu
- **Venue:** Interspeech 2021
- **arXiv:** 2104.03617 (submitted April 8, 2021; revised December 16, 2023)
- **DOI:** 10.21437/interspeech.2021-930

### Dataset Description

- Contains partially synthesized audio where specific words are replaced with synthesized versions using state-of-the-art speech synthesis
- Supports both **utterance-level detection** and **region-level localization**
- Key finding: "partially fake audio presents much more challenging than fully fake audio"
- Publicly available via Zenodo

### Role in Current Research

HAD serves as a standard cross-dataset evaluation benchmark alongside PartialSpoof. Recent papers consistently evaluate on both PartialSpoof and HAD to assess generalization.

## 6. State-of-the-Art Results on PartialSpoof (2024-2026)

### Utterance-Level Detection (EER)

| Method | Year | Venue | Utterance-level EER | Notes |
|--------|------|-------|---------------------|-------|
| **TDAM-AvgPool** (Li et al.) | 2025 | IEEE SPL | **0.59%** | Current SOTA. No frame-level supervision needed |
| PartialSpoof baseline (Zhang et al.) | 2023 | IEEE TASLP | 0.77% | Self-supervised features |
| Splicing artifact analysis (Negroni et al.) | 2024 | ASVspoof 5 Workshop | 6.16% | No trained detector; artifact analysis only |
| CFPRF (reproduced, Luong et al.) | 2025 | APSIPA 2025 | 7.61% (20 ms) | Frame-level model |
| SAL -- Segment-Aware Learning (Mao et al.) | 2026 | arXiv | Not reported in abstract | Strong in-domain and out-of-domain gains |

### Segment/Frame-Level Localization

| Method | Year | Venue | Segment-level EER | Notes |
|--------|------|-------|-------------------|-------|
| TDAM-AvgPool (Li et al.) | 2025 | IEEE SPL | Reported (full paper) | Temporal difference learning at multiple scales |
| CFPRF | 2025 | APSIPA 2025 | 7.61% (20 ms frame EER) | Coarse-to-fine pseudo-label refinement |
| SAL (Mao et al.) | 2026 | arXiv | "Notable gains in non-boundary regions" | Addresses over-reliance on boundary artifacts |
| TDL (Xie et al.) | 2023 | arXiv | Outperforms baselines | Embedding similarity + temporal convolution |

### HAD Dataset Results (for comparison)

| Method | Year | HAD Utterance-level EER |
|--------|------|------------------------|
| **TDAM-AvgPool** (Li et al.) | 2025 | **0.03%** |
| Splicing artifact analysis (Negroni et al.) | 2024 | 7.36% |
| CFPRF (Luong et al., reproduced) | 2025 | 14.98% |

### Key Observation

The gap between in-domain and out-of-domain performance is enormous:
- **Best in-domain (PartialSpoof):** 0.59% EER
- **Best on LlamaPartialSpoof (out-of-domain):** 24.49% EER
- **Best on HAD (cross-dataset):** 0.03% EER (TDAM) but 14.98% (CFPRF) -- suggesting model-specific generalization

## 7. State-of-the-Art Results on ADD 2023 Track 2 (2023-2025)

### ADD 2023 Challenge Overview

- **Paper:** "ADD 2023: the Second Audio Deepfake Detection Challenge"
- **Authors:** Jiangyan Yi, Jianhua Tao, Ruibo Fu, et al.
- **arXiv:** 2305.13774 (May 2023)
- **Track 2 task:** Manipulation Region Location (RL) -- localizing manipulated intervals in partially fake speech
- **Evaluation metric:** Combined ADD Score (weighted sentence accuracy + F1)

### Top System: DKU-DUKEECE (1st Place)

- **Paper:** "The DKU-DUKEECE System for the Manipulation Region Location Task of ADD 2023"
- **Authors:** Zexin Cai, Weiqing Wang, Yikang Wang, Ming Li
- **arXiv:** 2308.10281 (August 2023)

| Metric | Score |
|--------|-------|
| **Sentence Accuracy** | 82.23% |
| **F1 Score** | 60.66% |
| **Final ADD Score** | 0.6713 |

**System architecture:**
1. Dual frame-level models -- one for **boundary detection**, one for **deepfake segment detection**
2. VAE model trained exclusively on genuine audio for **authenticity verification**
3. Fusion of all three detection outputs

### Observations on ADD 2023 Track 2

- The F1 score of 60.66% (even for the winning system) indicates that precise localization of manipulation regions remains a substantially harder problem than utterance-level detection
- Sentence accuracy of 82.23% is reasonable but leaves ~18% of utterances with incorrect localization
- The gap between sentence accuracy and F1 suggests the system often gets the broad classification right but struggles with precise boundary delineation

## 9. Key Survey Paper

- **Title:** "Manipulated Regions Localization For Partially Deepfake Audio: A Survey"
- **Authors:** Jiayi He, Jiangyan Yi, Jianhua Tao, Siding Zeng, Hao Gu
- **Year:** 2025
- **arXiv:** 2506.14396 (submitted June 17, 2025; revised July 7, 2025)

This is the first systematic survey specifically on partially deepfake audio manipulated region localization. It covers:
- Fundamentals of the localization task
- Existing methods and their categorization
- Dataset landscape (PartialSpoof, HAD, LlamaPartialSpoof, HQ-MPSD, PartialEdit)
- Current limitations and emerging trends
- Evaluation metrics (EER, F1, precision, recall)

## 10. Research Gaps and Observations

### Gap 1: Generalization Failure
The most consistent finding across all recent work is **catastrophic generalization failure**. Models achieving <1% EER on PartialSpoof degrade to 24-43% EER on out-of-domain datasets (LlamaPartialSpoof, HQ-MPSD). This means current detection systems are essentially overfitting to dataset-specific artifacts rather than learning genuine manipulation indicators.

### Gap 2: Neural Speech Editing Detection
PartialEdit demonstrates that neural speech editing (VoiceCraft, A3T, FluentSpeech) produces manipulations that evade splice-boundary-based detectors entirely. This represents the next frontier of partial deepfake threats -- edits with no detectable boundaries.

### Gap 3: Multilingual Robustness
HQ-MPSD shows >80% performance degradation in cross-language evaluation, revealing that models learn language-specific rather than manipulation-specific features.

### Gap 4: Evaluation Metrics
Luong et al. (2025, arXiv 2507.03468) argue that EER is insufficient for real-world deployment and advocate for threshold-dependent metrics (accuracy, precision, recall, F1) that better reflect operational requirements.

### Gap 5: Boundary vs. Content Detection
SAL (Mao et al., 2026) and Liu et al. (2024) both demonstrate that current models **over-rely on boundary/transition artifacts** while neglecting the manipulated content itself. When artifacts are removed (as in HQ-MPSD and PartialEdit), detection collapses.

### Implications for Forensic/Judicial Applications

For court-admissible audio forensics:
1. **No single detector is reliable** across manipulation types -- an ensemble covering splice-based, editing-based, and content-based detection is necessary
2. **Frame-level localization** remains imprecise (ADD 2023 best F1 = 60.66%), which may be insufficient for legal certainty
3. **Transition-frame labeling** (as in HQ-MPSD) is forensically valuable because it explicitly marks uncertainty regions
4. **Cross-language generalization** is critical for international legal proceedings and is currently unsolved
5. **The attacker's perspective** (LlamaPartialSpoof) reveals that LLM-assisted editing creates a threat model that current CM systems cannot handle at forensic reliability levels
